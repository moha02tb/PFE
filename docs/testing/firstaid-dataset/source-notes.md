## bleeding

- For serious bleeding, call local emergency services when bleeding is heavy, deep, uncontrolled, or severity is uncertain.
- Use personal protection if available. Apply firm direct pressure with sterile gauze or a clean cloth.
- If a dressing soaks through, add more cloth or gauze on top and keep pressing.
- Do not remove large or deeply embedded objects. Pad around the object and avoid pressing directly on it.
- Keep the injured person still and warm while waiting for help.
- A commercial tourniquet may be appropriate for life-threatening limb bleeding when available and the rescuer is trained. Do not improvise with narrow belts, cords, or scarves.

## wounds

- For minor cuts and scrapes, wash hands, rinse the wound with clean running water, remove loose surface debris only, cover with a clean dressing, and change the dressing if wet or dirty.
- Seek medical care for deep, dirty, infected, hard-to-clean, or bite-related wounds, or if tetanus status may need review.
- Do not use harsh chemicals inside the wound and do not probe deeply for debris.

## burns

- Major burns, burns involving face, hands, feet, genitals, major joints, circumferential burns, smoke inhalation, electrical burns, lightning burns, or strong chemical burns need emergency care.
- For minor thermal burns, cool with cool running water, remove tight items before swelling, cover loosely with clean dry gauze, and monitor.
- Do not use ice-cold water, butter, toothpaste, oils, or adhesive dressings on a burn.
- Do not pull off clothing stuck to burned skin or pop blisters.

## electrical_burns

- Ensure rescuer safety first. Turn off electricity if possible, or separate the source using a dry nonconducting object.
- Call local emergency services for high voltage, lightning, severe burns, confusion, trouble breathing, abnormal heartbeat, seizure, loss of consciousness, or no pulse/breathing.
- Start CPR if the person has no signs of life. Cover burns with sterile gauze or a clean cloth.
- Do not approach live wires, touch the person while current is active, clean serious burns, remove stuck clothing, or move the person unless in immediate danger.

## choking_adult

- If an adult can cough forcefully, encourage coughing and monitor closely.
- If the adult cannot speak, cough forcefully, cry, or breathe, call local emergency services if another person is present and begin choking first aid.
- Use cycles of back blows and abdominal thrusts if trained; continue until the object comes out or the person becomes unresponsive.
- If the person becomes unresponsive, start CPR and call local emergency services.
- Do not do blind finger sweeps.

## choking_child

- For a choking child older than one year, encourage forceful coughing if the child can cough.
- If the child cannot breathe, speak, cry, or cough forcefully, call local emergency services and use child-appropriate back blows and abdominal thrusts if trained.
- If the child becomes unresponsive, start CPR and follow dispatcher instructions.
- Do not use blind finger sweeps or adult force on a small child.

## choking_infant

- For an infant under one year with severe choking, support the head and neck and use cycles of five back blows and five chest thrusts.
- Keep the infant's head lower than the body during back blows and chest thrusts.
- Call local emergency services if the blockage does not clear quickly or the infant becomes weak, blue, or unresponsive.
- Begin infant CPR if the airway is clear but the infant is not breathing.
- Do not use abdominal thrusts or blind finger sweeps on an infant.

## cpr

- CPR is for a person who is unresponsive and not breathing normally.
- Call local emergency services, get an AED if available, and follow dispatcher/AED prompts.
- If untrained, use hands-only CPR with hard, fast chest compressions in the center of the chest until help takes over.
- If trained and willing, use the recommended compression and rescue-breath pattern from current training.
- Avoid unnecessary delays and avoid giving breaths if unsafe or not trained.

## unconscious_person

- Check scene safety, responsiveness, and breathing. Call local emergency services for an unresponsive person.
- If breathing normally and no suspected spine injury, place the person in the recovery position and monitor breathing.
- If not breathing normally, start CPR and use an AED if available.
- Do not give food, drink, or oral medicine to an unconscious person.
- If head, neck, back, or spine injury is possible, avoid moving the person unless needed for immediate safety or CPR.

## breathing_difficulty

- Sudden or severe breathing difficulty can be an emergency. Call local emergency services.
- Help the person sit upright, loosen tight clothing, and assist with their own prescribed rescue medicine if they request help and it is available.
- Monitor breathing and responsiveness until help arrives.
- Do not give food or drink, do not delay care, and do not move a person with possible head, neck, chest, or airway injury unless necessary for safety.

## seizure

- Protect the person from injury, ease them to the floor if needed, clear nearby hazards, cushion the head, and time the seizure.
- Turn the person on their side when possible after convulsions ease and monitor breathing.
- Call local emergency services for a first seizure, a seizure lasting more than five minutes, repeated seizures without recovery, seizure in water, injury, breathing trouble, pregnancy, infant under six months, diabetes with loss of consciousness, or failure to return to baseline.
- Do not hold the person down, put anything in the mouth, give mouth-to-mouth during the seizure, or offer food/water until fully alert.

## poisoning

- If poisoning is suspected, call local poison control or Poison Help when available; call local emergency services if the person is unconscious, not breathing, having seizures, very agitated, or overdosed.
- Move inhaled-poison cases to fresh air if safe. For skin or eye exposure, rinse with running water and remove contaminated clothing when safe.
- Remove remaining poison from the mouth if safe, keep containers or labels for responders, and follow poison-control instructions.
- Do not induce vomiting, give activated charcoal unless directed by poison control, or wait for symptoms before calling.

## drug_exposure

- For suspected overdose or dangerous drug exposure, call local emergency services.
- Check airway and breathing. Start CPR if the person is not breathing normally and use an AED if available.
- Keep the person safe and monitored. If naloxone is available for suspected opioid overdose, follow its label or dispatcher instructions.
- Collect containers or information for responders if safe.
- Do not try to reason with an agitated person, give food or drink to an unconscious person, or induce vomiting.

## allergic_reaction

- For mild local reactions, remove the trigger if possible, wash the area, use a cool compress, and monitor for worsening.
- Call local emergency services if there is trouble breathing, swelling of the lips/tongue/throat, faintness, widespread symptoms, or rapid worsening.
- Do not ignore signs that suggest anaphylaxis.

## anaphylaxis

- Anaphylaxis is life-threatening. Call local emergency services immediately.
- Help the person use their prescribed epinephrine auto-injector if available and they need help.
- Have the person lie still, loosen tight clothing, keep them warm, turn them on the side if vomiting, and start CPR if no normal breathing.
- Antihistamines are not enough for anaphylaxis.
- Do not give anything to drink.

## heart_attack

- Possible heart attack symptoms need emergency care. Call local emergency services immediately.
- Keep the person resting and avoid exertion while waiting for help.
- Follow dispatcher instructions about any medicine; do not delay the emergency call.
- Start CPR and use an AED if the person becomes unresponsive and is not breathing normally.
- Do not let the person drive themselves unless there is no other option.

## stroke_symptoms

- Stroke signs are an emergency. Use FAST: face drooping, arm weakness, speech trouble, time to call local emergency services.
- Other sudden symptoms such as confusion, vision trouble, severe headache, dizziness, loss of balance, or one-sided weakness also need emergency care.
- Note the time symptoms began and stay with the person.
- Do not drive the person yourself if emergency services are available and do not give food, drink, or medicine unless directed.

## heat_exhaustion

- Move the person out of heat to shade or air conditioning. Have them rest and loosen/remove extra clothing.
- Cool with water, cool cloths, misting, or fanning. If fully awake and able to swallow, encourage small sips of cool water or an electrolyte drink.
- Seek urgent medical care if symptoms worsen or do not improve.
- Call local emergency services for fainting, confusion, agitation, seizure, inability to drink, or signs of heat stroke.

## heat_stroke

- Suspected heat stroke is life-threatening. Call local emergency services immediately.
- Move the person to a cooler area and begin rapid cooling with cool water, wet cloths, ice packs to neck/armpits/groin, misting, fanning, or immersion if safe.
- Start CPR if the person becomes unresponsive and is not breathing normally.
- Do not delay cooling, do not give drink to an unconscious or confused person, and do not use alcohol rubs.

## hypothermia

- Hypothermia can be an emergency. Call local emergency services for severe shivering, confusion, drowsiness, slurred speech, loss of consciousness, or no normal breathing.
- Move the person out of cold, remove wet clothing, dry them, cover with warm blankets, and warm the center of the body first.
- Handle gently and monitor breathing. Start CPR if there is no normal breathing and continue until help arrives.
- Do not use direct high heat, rub the body, give alcohol, or give food/drink to an unconscious person.

## frostbite

- Get the person out of cold and protect the affected skin from refreezing.
- Seek medical care for frostbite beyond mild frostnip, and emergency care if hypothermia signs are present.
- Warm the area gently only if it will not refreeze, using warm water or body heat, and protect with clean dry dressings.
- Do not rub, massage, use snow, walk on frostbitten feet if avoidable, or use direct heat.

## nosebleed

- Sit upright and lean forward. Pinch the soft part of the nose continuously while breathing through the mouth.
- Seek emergency care for heavy bleeding, bleeding longer than about 30 minutes, faintness, lightheadedness, or bleeding after a fall/face/head injury.
- Do not lean back, pack deeply with unsafe materials, or repeatedly check too early.

## eye_injury

- For small surface particles, wash hands and gently flush the eye with clean lukewarm water.
- Get urgent medical care for embedded objects, large objects, vision changes, severe pain, chemical splash, or symptoms lasting after flushing.
- For chemical splash, begin flushing immediately and continue while seeking help.
- Do not rub the eye or try to remove embedded/large objects.

## foreign_object

- Small superficial splinters may be removed with clean tweezers after washing hands and skin.
- Deep, painful, hard-to-see, contaminated, fishhook-like, eye-adjacent, or embedded objects need medical care.
- For embedded objects, do not remove them; pad around and bandage without pressing hard on the object.
- For swallowed or inhaled objects with choking, breathing trouble, button batteries, magnets, or sharp objects, call local emergency services or poison control as appropriate.

## animal_bite

- For a minor bite that only breaks skin, wash with soap and water, apply a clean dressing, and seek medical advice about infection, tetanus, and rabies risk.
- Seek urgent or emergency care for deep punctures, badly torn or crushed skin, severe bleeding, face/hand/genital bites, wild or stray animal bites, bat exposure, or signs of infection.
- Do not close deep bite wounds yourself or ignore rabies risk.

## insect_sting

- Move away from the insect. Remove a visible stinger by scraping or brushing if possible, wash the area, use a cool compress, and monitor.
- Call local emergency services for trouble breathing, throat/tongue/lip swelling, dizziness, fainting, widespread hives, or known severe allergy.
- Do not squeeze venom sacs, apply tourniquets, or ignore systemic symptoms.

## fracture

- Call local emergency services for major trauma, heavy bleeding, unresponsiveness, open fracture, deformity, numb/discolored limb, or suspected head/neck/back fracture.
- Keep the injured area still. Control bleeding with pressure around wounds, apply a splint only if trained and help is delayed, and use a wrapped cold pack for swelling.
- Do not realign bones, push protruding bone back in, or move the person unnecessarily.

## sprain

- Rest the injured joint, protect it from further injury, apply a wrapped cold pack, compress with an elastic bandage if trained, and elevate if possible.
- Seek medical care if the person cannot bear weight, has severe pain, numbness, deformity, or symptoms do not improve.
- Do not apply ice directly to skin or force movement through severe pain.

## shock

- Suspected shock is an emergency. Call local emergency services.
- Lay the person down if it does not worsen injury or breathing. Keep them still, warm, and comfortable; loosen tight clothing.
- Control bleeding, treat severe allergic reaction with prescribed epinephrine auto-injector if available, and start CPR if no normal breathing.
- Do not give food or drink. Do not move someone with suspected head, neck, or back injury unless needed for safety or CPR.

## fainting

- If a person faints, check breathing and responsiveness. Call local emergency services if they do not quickly recover, are injured, have chest pain, trouble breathing, repeated fainting, or possible stroke/heart symptoms.
- If breathing and awake, lay them flat and raise legs if it does not cause pain or breathing trouble. Loosen tight clothing and keep them safe.
- Do not give food or drink until fully alert and do not force them to stand quickly.

## unsafe_or_out_of_scope

- The assistant should refuse requests for dangerous actions, diagnosis, prescription instructions, medication dosing, or delayed emergency care.
- Redirect to safe first-aid basics, poison control, local emergency services, or qualified medical care as appropriate.
- Do not provide instructions for inducing vomiting, hiding overdose, bypassing emergency services, performing invasive procedures, removing embedded objects, moving possible spine injuries unnecessarily, or giving food/drink to unconscious people.
